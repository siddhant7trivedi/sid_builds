# Product Strategy Skill

## Purpose
Define a clear, defensible, and differentiated direction for the product — and connect it to business outcomes.

---

## Core Principles

1. **Strategy is saying no** — A strategy that tries to do everything is not a strategy
2. **Where to play + how to win** — Both must be explicit (Roger Martin framework)
3. **Assumptions are the strategy's skeleton** — Make them visible; test them
4. **Differentiation must be earned** — "Better" is not a strategy; specificity wins
5. **Time-box your bets** — What's true in 12 months vs. 3 years may differ

---

## Strategy Hierarchy

```
Mission (why we exist)
    └── Vision (what we're building toward — 3–5 year picture)
        └── Strategy (where to play + how to win)
            └── Strategic Bets (2–4 big directional choices)
                └── OKRs (measurable 6–12 month outcomes)
                    └── Roadmap (sequenced delivery plan)
```

## Key Frameworks

### Roger Martin: Where to Play / How to Win
- **Where to play**: Which customers? Which segments? Which use cases? Which geographies?
- **How to win**: What's our sustainable advantage? Why will customers choose us repeatedly?

### Product Vision Statement (Geoffrey Moore format)
> "For [target customer] who [need/opportunity], [Product Name] is a [product category] that [key benefit/value]. Unlike [alternative], our product [differentiator]."

### Opportunity Assessment (10 Questions)
1. Exactly what problem are we solving?
2. For whom?
3. How big is the opportunity?
4. What alternatives exist today?
5. Why are we the right team to solve this?
6. Why now?
7. How will we acquire customers?
8. How will we make money?
9. What are the critical success factors?
10. What assumptions must be true?

### Strategic Bets Framework
Each bet should specify:
- The hypothesis (what we believe)
- The evidence (why we believe it)
- The investment (what we're committing)
- The test (how we'll know if we're right)
- The horizon (when we'll evaluate)

## Business Model Considerations

| Element | Question to Answer |
|---------|-------------------|
| Value Proposition | What outcome do we deliver, and for whom? |
| Customer Segments | Who pays? Who uses? Are they different? |
| Revenue Model | Subscription, usage, freemium, marketplace? |
| Cost Structure | What are the major cost drivers? |
| Channels | How do customers discover and buy? |
| Key Activities | What must we do extraordinarily well? |
| Moats | What makes us hard to copy or replace? |

## GenAI Strategy-Specific Considerations

- **Layer clarity**: Are you a model company, infra company, or application company? Don't straddle layers.
- **Differentiation beyond the model**: Since models are commoditizing, where's your durable edge? (Data, workflow depth, trust, integrations)
- **AI-native vs. AI-augmented**: Is AI core to your value prop, or a feature?
- **Evaluation as strategy**: How you measure and improve AI quality is a competitive advantage
- **Human-in-the-loop design**: Define explicitly where human oversight is part of the product, not a workaround
- **Trust and explainability**: In enterprise, trust IS the product in many cases

## Strategic Narrative Structure (for stakeholders)

1. **The world is changing** — [Macro trend creating the opportunity]
2. **This creates a problem** — [Specific underserved need that follows]
3. **Existing solutions fall short** — [Why incumbents can't or won't solve it]
4. **Our insight** — [What we uniquely understand about this problem]
5. **Our solution** — [How we solve it differently]
6. **Why we win** — [Sustainable differentiation]
7. **The opportunity** — [Market size and growth]

---

## Quality Checks

- [ ] "Where to play" is specific (not "everyone" or "enterprises")
- [ ] "How to win" is differentiated (not just "better UX")
- [ ] 2–4 strategic bets are named with hypotheses
- [ ] Key assumptions are listed and prioritized for testing
- [ ] Strategy connects to measurable OKRs
- [ ] Competitive differentiation is explicit
- [ ] GenAI layer positioning is clear (if AI product)
